## Introduction

Security controls are safeguards or countermeasures designed to protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information and systems. Controls can be categorized by their **type** (administrative, physical, technical) and their **function** (preventive, detective, deterrent, corrective).

### The Control Matrix

| Function             | Administrative         | Physical                       | Technical                    |
| :------------------- | :--------------------- | :----------------------------- | :--------------------------- |
| **Preventive** | Policies, procedures   | Gates, locks, fences           | Antivirus, firewalls, MFA    |
| **Detective**  | Audits, reviews        | Cameras, motion sensors        | IDS, SIEM, honeypots         |
| **Deterrent**  | Consequences, training | Guards, lighting, signage      | Warning banners, agreements  |
| **Corrective** | BCP, IR plans          | Repair controls, reissue cards | Patching, quarantine, reboot |

├─ IPS detects attack pattern

## Key Takeaways

### Control Types

- **Administrative**: Policies, procedures, training, plans (the "paper" controls)
- **Physical**: Tangible barriers, locks, cameras, guards (the "physical" controls)
- **Technical**: Software, hardware, configurations (the "technology" controls)

### Control Functions

- **Preventive**: Stop incidents before they occur
- **Detective**: Identify incidents that have occurred
- **Deterrent**: Discourage potential attackers
- **Corrective**: Fix and recover after incidents

### Defense in Depth

Multiple layers of controls ensure that if one fails, others provide protection.

### Key Principles

1. No single control is sufficient
2. Controls should be layered and integrated
3. Match controls to risks and assets
4. Test controls to ensure they work
5. Update controls as threats evolve
