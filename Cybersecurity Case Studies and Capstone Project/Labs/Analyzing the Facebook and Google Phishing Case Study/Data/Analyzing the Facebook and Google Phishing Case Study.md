# Analyzing the Facebook and Google Phishing Case Study (2013-2015)

## Case Study Overview

The phishing attack on Facebook and Google from 2013 to 2015 serves as a critical case study, showcasing how even the largest and most resourceful organizations can fall victim to sophisticated phishing scams. This case study shows how **Evaldas Rimasauskas** exploited vulnerabilities in invoicing processes, using social engineering and email spoofing to defraud the companies of **over $100 million**.

By analyzing this case, you can gain valuable information about the necessity of robust security protocols, employee awareness, and advanced detection tools to prevent similar threats.

## Complete Case Study Analysis

| Section                                         | Details                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| :---------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Root Cause**                            | **Initial point of failure:** The phishing scam, orchestrated by Evaldas Rimasauskas between 2013 and 2015, exploited weaknesses in invoicing processes at Facebook and Google. Rimasauskas created a fake company that mimicked Quanta Computer, a legitimate Taiwanese electronics manufacturer, and sent fraudulent invoices to both companies.`<br><br>`**Factors contributing to the vulnerability:**`<br>`• **Lack of verification mechanisms:** Both companies lacked strict verification mechanisms for validating the authenticity of invoices and payment requests.`<br>`• **Social engineering:** Rimasauskas effectively used social engineering techniques to deceive employees, exploiting their trust and established business processes.`<br>`• **Email spoofing:** The attacker forged email addresses to appear as if they came from Quanta Computer, making the fraudulent requests appear legitimate.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| **Actions Taken**                         | •**Internal detection:** Employees at Google and Facebook eventually noticed discrepancies in financial records and flagged suspicious payment requests.`<br>`• **Tracing payments:** The companies traced fraudulent payments made to bank accounts in Latvia and Cyprus.`<br>`• **Law enforcement collaboration:** Both companies worked with international authorities to gather evidence, track the perpetrator, and ultimately arrest Evaldas Rimasauskas in 2017.`<br>`• **Legal prosecution:** Rimasauskas was extradited to the United States, pleaded guilty to wire fraud in 2019, and was sentenced to 5 years in prison.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| **Evaluate Effectiveness and Timeliness** | **Evaluation:** The internal detection of discrepancies was **not timely**, as the fraudulent transactions remained undetected and continued for nearly **two years** (2013-2015). This delay allowed the attacker to defraud the companies of over $100 million before any alarm was raised.`<br><br>`However, **once detected**, the organizations were timely and effective in initiating further action. The response demonstrated strong incident response capabilities:`<br>`• Rapid engagement of law enforcement`<br>`• Successful international coordination`<br>`• Full recovery of approximately $50 million (nearly half the stolen funds)`<br>`• Successful prosecution of the perpetrator                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| **Successes, Gaps, and Failures**         | **Successes:**`<br>`• Once detected, the fraudulent activities were promptly reported to authorities.`<br>`• Successful collaboration with law enforcement agencies (FBI, international partners) led to the arrest and prosecution of the perpetrator.`<br>`• Approximately $50 million of the stolen funds were recovered.`<br><br>`**Gaps:**`<br>`• The lack of verification mechanisms allowed fraudulent invoices to be processed without initial detection for nearly two years.`<br>`• Insufficient training in recognizing and handling sophisticated phishing attempts contributed to the scam's success.`<br>`• No multi-factor authentication or secondary approval process for high-value payments.`<br><br>`**Failures:**`<br>`• The initial vulnerability in the invoicing processes was a critical failure that allowed the scam to occur.`<br>`• Inadequate segregation of duties in the accounts payable process.`<br>`• Failure to verify vendor banking information changes through out-of-band communication.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| **Impact on the Organization**            | **Short-term impacts:**`<br>`• **Financial losses:** Immediate financial losses due to fraudulent payments (over $100 million total).`<br>`• **Operational disruptions:** Temporary disruptions in financial operations as the companies investigated and responded to the incident.`<br>`• **Legal costs:** Legal fees and communications resulted from the phishing attack.`<br><br>`**Long-term impacts:**`<br>`• **Process changes:** Both companies implemented sweeping changes to their accounts payable verification processes.`<br>`• **Security investments:** Increased investment in fraud detection tools and employee training.`<br>`• **Industry influence:** The case became a benchmark for business email compromise (BEC) awareness across the technology sector.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| **Lessons Learned**                       | **Lesson #1: Importance of verification** - There is a critical need for stringent verification mechanisms for invoices and payment requests, especially for high-value transactions. Organizations cannot rely solely on email as a trusted communication channel.`<br><br>`**Lesson #2: Employee training saves millions** - Regular, ongoing training in recognizing and responding to phishing attempts and social engineering tactics is essential. The employees who eventually detected the fraud did so because of their attention to detail—this skill can be taught and reinforced.`<br><br>`**Lesson #3: Collaborative efforts work** - The effectiveness of collaborating with law enforcement agencies in addressing cybercrime cannot be overstated. International cooperation led to the arrest, prosecution, and partial asset recovery.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| **Recommendations for Future Actions**    | **DO implement:**`<br>`• **Multi-layer verification:** Establish multiple layers of verification for all financial transactions, including out-of-band confirmation (e.g., phone call to known vendor contact) for any changes to banking information.`<br>`• **Regular employee training:** Conduct mandatory, frequent training sessions on cybersecurity awareness and phishing detection, including simulated phishing exercises.`<br>`• **Strengthen incident response plans:** Develop and regularly update incident response plans specifically for financial fraud scenarios, with clear escalation paths.`<br>`• **Use advanced threat detection tools:** Deploy machine learning and AI tools to detect and block suspicious activities, including anomaly detection in payment patterns.`<br><br>`**DO NOT implement:**`<br>`• **Do not rely solely on email verification** - Never approve high-value payments based exclusively on email requests.`<br>`• **Do not assume size equals security** - Both Google and Facebook were sophisticated organizations; complacency affects all companies regardless of resources.                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| **Conclusion**                            | The Google and Facebook phishing attacks (2013-2015) highlight the critical importance of robust cybersecurity measures and proactive incident response, regardless of organizational size or resources. Despite being two of the most technologically sophisticated companies in the world, both fell victim to a relatively simple business email compromise (BEC) scheme because of basic process failures.`<br><br>`This case underscores the need for organizations to continuously evolve their security practices, recognizing that human processes are often the weakest link. The lessons learned from this incident serve as a valuable guide for other organizations in strengthening their defenses against similar threats.`<br><br>`**Broader implications:** Business Email Compromise (BEC) remains one of the costliest cybercrimes. According to the FBI IC3 report, BEC attacks resulted in over $2.9 billion in losses in 2023 alone. The Facebook-Google case remains the canonical example of how even industry leaders can be victimized when basic verification controls are absent.`<br><br>`**Two key references:**`<br>`1. U.S. Department of Justice. (2019, March). Lithuanian man sentenced for wire fraud scheme that defrauded Google and Facebook. `<br>`2. FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3). (2024). 2023 Internet Crime Report. |
